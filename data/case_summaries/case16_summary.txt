**Case Name:** Abdul Sattar v. Ms. Kalsoom  
**Court:** Sindh High Court  
**Year:** 2006  

**Legal Issue:** Whether a Family Court may pass a preliminary decree for dissolution of marriage on the ground of Khula when pre‑trial proceedings fail, and the dower (Haq Mahr) is not disputed, and how disputed dower or other reliefs affect the court’s discretion.  

**Relevant Law / Sections:**  
- West Pakistan Family Courts Act, 1964 – Sections 5, 7(2), 10(4) (proviso), 12(a), 17  
- Proviso to Section 10(4) (dissolution upon failed pre‑trial)  
- Islamic law on Khula (remission of dower)  
- Bridal Gifts and Dowry Articles Act, 2004 (non‑return of gifts)  

**Key Facts (brief):**  
1. Respondent filed Family Suit No. 77 of 2005 for dissolution of marriage on the basis of Khula.  
2. Pre‑trial proceedings between the parties failed; the Family Court had not yet decreed the suit.  
3. A transfer application to move the suit to another district was dismissed.  
4. The respondent argued that, per the proviso to Section 10(4), the court should have decreed dissolution because dower was not disputed.  
5. Amicus Curiae (Abdul Qadir Shaikh) explained that Khula requires only remission of dower; gifts are not to be returned.  

**Holding / Decision:**  
The court laid down that:  
- When pre‑trial fails and dower is undisputed, the Family Court must decree dissolution of marriage on the ground of Khula.  
- If dower is disputed, a preliminary decree may be passed only if the wife deposits the dower in court; otherwise the matter must be decided after evidence.  
- The same rule applies when the suit seeks additional reliefs (custody, maintenance, dowry articles).  
- Gifts other than dower need not be returned to the husband.  

**Legal Principle Established:**  
A Family Court may issue a preliminary decree for dissolution of marriage on the basis of Khula when pre‑trial fails and dower is not contested; if dower is contested, a preliminary decree is permissible only upon the wife’s deposit of the dower, otherwise the court must resolve the dispute after evidence. This principle clarifies the application of the proviso to Section 10(4) of the West Pakistan Family Courts Act, 1964.
